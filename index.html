From the Deep Dive

Engineering decisions behind the Teams agentic AI platform

The full write-up is a story of load-bearing decisions — what I reused, what I built, the tradeoffs I lived with, and what I'd do differently. A few of the calls:

Reuse, deliberately

Thin model interface over Azure OpenAI + Claude

Never entertained training our own — model quality shifts month-to-month. The abstraction stayed thin: swap providers, tier routing by complexity (−40% cost/request), unify telemetry. Explicitly not a framework.

Build, because nothing fit

A meeting-shaped orchestration runtime

Open frameworks were single-process, single-tenant, batch-shaped. We needed distributed, low-latency, turn-taking that respects the floor, idempotent retries, and partial-failure isolation so one bad agent can't take down the meeting.

The piece I'm proudest of

A declarative agent manifest schema

Code-first SDKs assumed engineers. Ours targeted PMs and IT admins too — specify intent, retrieval surfaces, permissions, HITL defaults, and the runtime fills in correct behavior. Reliability +3×, support tickets −60%, because the right defaults were locked into the shape.

What I'd do differently

The eval harness should have come first

We shipped the runtime to GA before a real eval harness and caught regressions in production an eval suite would have flagged on commit. Now: eval as a P0 deliverable alongside the first model integration — delay the irreversible, accelerate the reversible.
